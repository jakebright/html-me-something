Though I have been through a lot in life, there is so much more still to come.

Marquette High School

For me, high school was not the best four years of my life. It was a lot of hard work, and dedication to keep me going everyday and some days I didn't. Don't get me wrong it was fun, I had a select group of friends and soccer. Everyday I worked my butt off to improve the skills that I needed in order to get a scholarship to play soccer at a higher level. With this took sacrifice, and I didnt't always make the party on Saturday night, but I was alright with that. As long as it gave me the opportunity to "level up" in what I thought was the most important aspect of my life, soccer. Boy was I wrong.

Universities

On the journey to being educated, Maryville University was my first stop. I did recieve the scholarship that I desperately wanted, however, Now there was there was nothing to look forward to. I didn't buckle down on books and class, rather it turned into a big party for me. Class was second fiddle to my newly acquired friends. Soccer was important, but I got burnecd out on it. The one good thing that came out of going to Maryville was definitely not my grades, but I did get close with an old friend from high school and she is now my wife. Another good thing that came from it, in hindsight, is that I was forced to grow up and deal with consequences of not doing well in school. My next stop was a year off and community college.

The aforementioned year off was the hardest year of my life. I was in a near death accident on the beach of Destin, Florida, and after a couple surgeries, a lot of pain, physical therapy, relearning how to walk, and the best girlfriend by my side, I decided to give school another shot. After all I wasn't doing anything on the couch at home. I hit the books hard at Wildwood, got straight As, made the Dean's List, and tried to recieve my Associates of Arts in Teaching. Howver, this did not happen, with transferring credits from Maryville, I was still short a hand full of credits to recieve this degree. I was discouraged, I didn't want to keep going to community college for only a few credits. So instead of sticking it out and taking the one or two classes that I needed I decided to transfer. With no associates degree, I applied to UMSL.

UMSL was a good spot for me. I worked and went to school during this time. It was topugh, but I made it work. I was studying a degree in teaching arts. I wanted to become a Special Education Teacher. The work load wasn't anything that I couldn't handle, however, the time commitment with work and with all of the shadow hours each week got to where I was unable to work a full time job and continue school. See, in order to become a teach I would have had to been in a classroom forty hours a week, without working the job that I had at the time. Which I was working around fifty hours a week at. I decided to take a semester off, shortly after, and never went back.

Occupations

One of two jobs that I have had during my adult life was at The Gatesworth, a retirement home for the super wealthy of basically the entire country. People would come from all over to live here. It was not cheap to live here either. I had three main jobs, Doorman, Dog Walker, and Concierge, which is fancy for "do wahtever anyone asks me to do." This was a fun job, between the people I worked with and the residence that I got to know I had a blast. However, the money that I made was not nearly enough to make ends meet. Granted, I was only 20-22 with rent and beer to worry about, but I wanted more. Eventually, like most jobs that come and go, I became discouraged and realized there was more to life then serving hand and foot to old people. I found a new jobe, and quit.

My current job, John Bender Drywall and Painting. I quit The Gateworth in order to become a one-ton truck driver, drywall hanger, service order worker, estimator, and laborer for a drywall company here in St. Louis. This was the best and worst decision I think I have ever made. The best, because I have never made the amount of money that I make, the worst because this type of work is not sustainable for you body. It is hard, back breaking labor in which most people after twenty years are broke down and can hardly move. Looking at the older guys that I work with shows me that I cannot stay in this industry. Most are unhappy, divorced, borderline alcoholics that hate what they are doing. i.e. drywall. I don't exactly like my job. I work too much, and there is more to life than just work.

That is where LaunchCode comes into play. For me LaunchCode is like a way out. I feel stuck with drywall, and LaunchCode makes me look forward to something new and exciting. Though it is extre,ely tough at times, I know that if I grit my teeth and get the work done I will be learning a skill that could land me a job in tech. Like drywall, Tech isn't going anywhere. Unlike drywall, tech is not back breaking. It may be mind breaking, but we won't get into that. The challenge of being able to create something from nothing just using a computer used to be unfathomable, now I can see myself creating. Like an artist taking a blank canvas and turning it into something that people, hopefully, enjoy. Sometimes in life you have to take chances, and this is a chance that I am willing to take. Not only for myself, but for my wife as well. She is the one that suggested I look into this course, and I am 100% commited to finishing this course, going onto an apprenticeship, and fingers crossed being a successfull computer programmer.

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu

Jiu-Jitsu is a major part of my life. I started training at the end of 2015, and for the last two years have been to the gym at least five days a week. It is a grind, but it's a good grind. I have met a lot of awesome people. My coaches really know there stuff, and there is more to learn than you or I could imagine. After two years, I have earned my blue belt. The belt system works by your coach basically deciding when you are ready to move on. There is no set time or even attendance level that will get you there quicker. It all depends on whether you put in the work and earn it yourself. Nothing is given to you. The next belt that I will earn is the purple belt. Then brown and finally my black belt. The process usually takes at least ten years to finish, and once you become a black belt the learning really starts to begin. I assume, they say this because jiu-jitsu truely is a life skill. I suggest that everyone does it. It is the most fun I have had in my adult life.
